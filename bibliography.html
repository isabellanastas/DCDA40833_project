Source 1

“Aesthetic–Usability Effect.” Wikipedia, Wikimedia Foundation, 19 Mar. 2026, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aesthetic%E2%80%93usability_effect.

Annotation: This source connects to my research question by explaining how people tend to perceive visually appealing things as more valuable or effective. It helped me understand why aesthetic content might seem higher quality, even if that is not always true. The concept expands on my topic by providing an explanation for why visuals influence decision-making. It also connects to broader research in design and user experience. It raises questions about how often people rely on appearance rather than substance when making choices. One limitation is that it is more general and not specific to food or wellness. I plan to use this to support my argument that aesthetics influence perception and consumer behavior.

Source 2

Casales-Garcia, Vicente, et al. “Analyzing Aesthetics, Attractiveness and Color of Gastronomic Images for User Engagement.” ScienceDirect, June 2025, www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1389041725000385.

Annotation: This source is relevant to my topic because it shows that visually appealing food images tend to get more engagement and more positive reactions. It helped me understand that aesthetics don’t just attract attention, but can also influence how people perceive food, including whether they think it is healthier or higher quality. It raises questions about whether people are choosing food based more on how it looks rather than its actual value. One limitation is that it focuses more on engagement than long-term behavior. I plan to use this source in my food section to support my argument that aesthetics play a major role in shaping food choices.

Source 3

Johnson, Prince Ghuman & Matt. “How Perception Influences Food Aesthetics and Healthiness.” Pop Neuro, Pop Neuro, 3 May 2022, www.popneuro.com/neuromarketing-blog/how-perception-influences-food-aesthetics-and-healthiness.

Annotation: This source is especially important for my project because it explains how people often associate visually appealing food with being healthier. It directly supports my argument that aesthetics can shape perception, even if that perception is not accurate. It expands on the topic by connecting design and visual appeal to decision-making and consumer behavior. It raises questions about how often consumers rely on visual cues instead of actual information when making choices. A limitation is that it is more of an overview than a detailed research study. I plan to use this to explain the psychology behind why aesthetic food is so influential.

Source 4

Pantic, Igor. “Online Social Networking and Mental Health.” Cyberpsychology, Behavior and Social Networking, U.S. National Library of Medicine, Oct. 2014, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4183915/.

Annotation: This article connects to my research question by examining how social media influences body image and perceptions of health. It helped me understand that the content people see online, especially highly curated and aesthetic posts, can shape how they view their own lifestyles. The article expands on the topic by showing that constant exposure to idealized images can lead to comparison and pressure to meet certain standards. It raises questions about whether aesthetic wellness content is motivating or harmful. I plan to use this to support my argument that aesthetic culture shapes both behavior and self-perception.

Source 5

Paul, Bernadette, and Sely-Ann Headley-Johnson. “The Impact of Social Media on Health Behaviors, a Systematic Review.” Healthcare (Basel, Switzerland), U.S. National Library of Medicine, 30 Oct. 2025, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12608964/.

Annotation: This article connects directly to my research question because it explains how social media influences behaviors like diet, exercise, and overall wellness choices. It helped me better understand that repeated exposure to certain types of content can shape what people see as ideal in terms of health. The article pulls from multiple studies, which makes it strong for my project. It also raises questions about whether these influences are actually beneficial or if they can create unrealistic expectations. I plan to use this source to support my claim that social media plays a major role in shaping how people define and approach health.

Source 6

“Processing Fluency Theory of Aesthetic Pleasure.” Wikipedia, Wikimedia Foundation, 22 Feb. 2026, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Processing_fluency_theory_of_aesthetic_pleasure.

Annotation: This source helps explain the psychological reasoning behind my topic. It connects to my research by showing that people naturally prefer things that are visually easy to process, which often translates to what we consider aesthetic. It helped me better understand why aesthetic food and wellness content is so appealing and why it feels more desirable. It raises questions about how much of our decision-making is influenced by visuals. One limitation is that it is more theoretical and not specific to food or wellness. I plan to use this to explain why aesthetics are so powerful in shaping behavior.

Source 7

Sachu, Berlin, et al. “Impact of Instagram Food Influencers on Eating Habits and Food Preferences among Social Media Users.” ResearchGate, July 2025, www.researchgate.net/publication/396363846_Impact_of_Instagram_Food_Influencers_on_Eating_Habits_and_Food_Preferences_among_Social_Media_Users.

Annotation: This source connects to my topic by showing how influencers impact people’s food preferences and eating behaviors. It helped me understand how trust and relatability play a role in shaping consumer decisions, especially when content feels personal or aesthetic. It raises questions about how much influence content creators actually have and how that affects long-term habits. I plan to use this source to connect my discussion of aesthetic content with the role of creators.

Source 8

Sajjad, Amber, and Fahad Mansoor Pasha. “Beauty and the Beast: An Experimental Study on the Effect of Instagram Food Posts Featuring a Celebrity vs. without a Celebrity on the Eating Intention of Healthy vs. Unhealthy Food.” BMC Public Health, U.S. National Library of Medicine, 24 Nov. 2025, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12642246/.

Annotation: This article connects to my research question by showing how social media content, especially from influencers, can impact what people choose to eat. It expands on the idea that exposure to certain foods online can directly influence real-life decisions. I found it helpful because it highlights the relationship between content, trust, and behavior. It also raises questions about how much control people actually have over their choices if they are constantly influenced by what they see online. One limitation is that it focuses more on influencer impact rather than aesthetics specifically. I plan to use this to connect aesthetic content with actual behavior and decision-making.

Source 9

Shi, Aiqin, et al. “Effects of Design Aesthetics on the Perceived Value of a Product.” Frontiers in Psychology, U.S. National Library of Medicine, 29 July 2021, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8359925/.

Annotation: This source connects to my topic by explaining how design aesthetics influence the way consumers perceive value. It helped me better understand that people often see visually appealing products as better or more desirable, even before they know much else about them. The article expands on this idea by looking at how aesthetics affect consumer processing. It raises questions about how often people rely on appearance rather than actual quality when making decisions. I plan to use this to support my argument that aesthetics strongly shape consumer perception and behavior.

Source 10

Zanden, Demi van der. “The Aesthetics of Wellness: How Instagram Shapes Happiness and Exclusion.” Diggit Magazine, www.diggitmagazine.com/aesthetics-wellness-how-instagram-shapes-happiness-and-exclusion. Accessed 24 Mar. 2026.

Annotation: This article connects closely to my research question because it focuses on how Instagram shapes ideas of wellness through specific aesthetics and trends. It helped me better understand how platforms promote a certain image of what a “healthy” lifestyle looks like, often tied to visuals like minimalism, routines, and curated content. The article expands on the topic by discussing how these ideals can create both aspiration and exclusion. It also references other cultural and media studies, which could be useful for further research. It raises questions about whether these representations are realistic or if they pressure people to meet certain standards. I plan to use this to support my argument that wellness has become a curated identity online.
