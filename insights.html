Final Insights

Conclusions, Implications & Future Research

Executive Summary

This project explored how social media aesthetics shape perceptions of health, specifically how visual trends on platforms like TikTok and Instagram influence what people interpret as "healthy." Through survey research, case study analysis, and secondary source review, this project examined how aesthetic presentation impacts perceptions of credibility, desirability, and wellness.

The findings showed a clear pattern: people often associate visual polish with health value. Survey respondents consistently chose the more aesthetic option as healthier, more desirable, and higher quality, even when the actual nutritional difference was unclear. Across both survey and case study findings, aesthetics functioned as a shortcut for trust and perceived wellness.

This research suggests that on social media, health is often judged less by substance and more by presentation. As wellness content becomes increasingly visual, the way health is packaged online plays a major role in shaping how audiences interpret and engage with it.

Key Findings

This research reveals several important findings about how health and wellness are represented on social media:

  • Finding 1: Aesthetic presentation strongly shapes health perception, with 100% of respondents choosing the more visually polished option as healthier.
  • Finding 2: People are more likely to choose what looks better, as 93.5% said they would prefer to eat the more aesthetic option.
  • Finding 3: Visual appeal often defines what people consider healthy, with 80.6% saying they chose their preferred option simply because it looked better.
  • Finding 4: Social media plays a major role in shaping food and wellness decisions, with 100% of respondents saying they use it for recommendations.

Conclusions

This research revealed a strong relationship between social media aesthetics and health perception. Across both survey responses and social media case studies, participants consistently associated visually polished content with being healthier, more desirable, and more trustworthy. Rather than relying on nutritional value or factual information, respondents often used visual cues like styling, color, and presentation to make judgments about health.

A clear pattern emerged across the data: aesthetics strongly shape perception. The survey results showed that respondents were overwhelmingly drawn to the more curated and visually appealing option, while the case studies reinforced how platforms like TikTok and Instagram reward aesthetic presentation through engagement and visibility. Together, these findings support the original research question by showing that social media aesthetics do not just influence what people like, but also what they believe is healthy.

Answering the Research Question

Research Question: How do visual trends on platforms like TikTok and Instagram shape perceptions of what is "healthy" in food and wellness?

Based on this research, visual trends on social media strongly shape what people perceive as healthy by influencing how health is presented, interpreted, and trusted. Users often rely on visual cues like clean styling, color palettes, minimalism, and polished presentation as indicators of wellness, even when those cues are not tied to actual nutritional value.

The strongest evidence supporting this conclusion came from the survey results, where respondents consistently chose the more aesthetic option as healthier and more desirable. Case study findings further supported this by showing that highly curated health content often performs better and is interpreted as more credible. Together, these findings suggest that on social media, aesthetics often shape health perception more than substance.

Implications & Applications

What are the implications of this research for different audiences?

For Young People & Social Media Users

Young people should recognize that health content on social media is often designed to look credible before it is actually informative. A polished aesthetic can make content feel healthier or more trustworthy, even when it lacks context or accuracy. Media literacy is essential in helping users question what they are seeing, evaluate content more critically, and separate visual appeal from actual health value. While social media can make wellness content more engaging and accessible, it also increases the risk of misinformation, unrealistic standards, and surface-level understandings of health.

For Health Communicators & Educators

This research shows that aesthetics matter in health communication, but they should support information rather than replace it. Public health professionals and educators can learn from the visual strategies used in wellness content by making information more engaging, accessible, and visually digestible. However, this also highlights a gap between what performs well on social media and what is scientifically accurate. Health information online should be communicated in ways that are both visually effective and evidence-based.

For Social Media Platforms

Social media platforms play a major role in shaping how health content is distributed and interpreted. Algorithms often reward highly aesthetic wellness content because it performs well visually, which can amplify surface-level or misleading health messaging. Platforms have a responsibility to consider how health content is promoted, moderated, and contextualized. Greater transparency, stronger moderation, and more visible sourcing could help reduce the spread of misleading wellness content.

For Content Creators & Influencers

Creators have significant influence over how audiences interpret health and wellness online. While aesthetics can make content more engaging, creators should be mindful of the responsibility that comes with presenting health-related information. Ethical content creation requires balancing visual appeal with honesty, context, and accuracy. Creators should recognize that audiences often interpret polished content as credible, which makes transparency especially important.

Limitations of This Research

This research has several limitations that should be acknowledged. First, the survey sample size was relatively small, with 31 responses, which limits how broadly the findings can be generalized. The sample also reflected a narrow demographic, primarily college-aged participants, making the results most relevant to younger social media users.

This project also focused primarily on TikTok- and Instagram-style content, meaning findings may not apply as directly to other platforms with different formats or audience behaviors. Because the survey measured perception rather than actual behavior, it captured how people interpret health content rather than how they act on it in real life.

Additionally, responses were shaped by subjective interpretation, meaning outside factors such as personal habits, media literacy, prior beliefs, and familiarity with wellness trends may have influenced results.

Directions for Future Research

Future research could expand this project by studying a larger and more diverse sample across age groups, demographics, and social media habits. This would help determine whether these patterns are specific to younger users or reflect broader digital behavior.

Additional research could also compare how health aesthetics function across different platforms, such as YouTube, Pinterest, or Snapchat, where content is consumed differently. Longitudinal research would also be useful in understanding how these visual trends evolve over time and how repeated exposure shapes health beliefs.

Future studies could also explore how media literacy interventions affect the way users interpret aesthetic health content. It would be valuable to examine whether teaching users to critically evaluate wellness content changes how they perceive credibility, trust, and health online.

Reflections on the Research Process

This project showed me how much visual presentation influences the way people interpret health online. What surprised me most was not just that aesthetics mattered, but how consistently people relied on them to define what looked healthy. I expected visuals to influence preference, but it was more striking to see how strongly they shaped credibility and trust.

One of the biggest challenges in this project was translating a broad idea into something measurable and structured. Because the topic is so visual and subjective, it was challenging to build a project that balanced research, design, and interpretation. At the same time, that challenge made the process more interesting and helped me think more critically about how digital media shapes everyday perception.

Final Thoughts

The most important takeaway from this project is that on social media, health is often judged by how it looks before what it actually is. Visual aesthetics shape not only what people click on, but what they trust, prefer, and interpret as healthy.

As health and wellness content becomes increasingly tied to digital culture, it is important to recognize that visual appeal is not the same thing as credibility. What looks healthy online is not always what is healthiest in practice, and understanding that difference is essential to navigating digital wellness culture more critically.
